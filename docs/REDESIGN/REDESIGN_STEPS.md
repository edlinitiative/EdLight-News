Here’s a proper UX audit document for news.edlight.org, focused on what to update to make the product feel more polished, premium, and interactive.

Executive summary

The site already has a strong product foundation: clear content pillars, useful student-first framing, bilingual intent, and several specialized surfaces like bourses, calendrier, universités, parcours, histoire, and succès. The main weakness is not structure, it is experience quality. Across the site, many pages rely on similar text-heavy sections, similar card weight, and limited interaction depth, so the interface feels more functional than premium.  ￼

The biggest opportunities are:
	•	stronger visual hierarchy
	•	more distinctive page personalities
	•	a tighter card system
	•	better state design for filters, sorting, saves, urgency, and exploration
	•	cleaner spacing and typography
	•	richer interaction patterns without making the UI busy  ￼

Severity scale

I’m using:
	•	Critical: hurts perceived quality immediately
	•	High: noticeably limits polish and usability
	•	Medium: solid page, but premium feel is missing
	•	Low: not urgent, mostly refinement

1) /news — Critical

This is the page that most needs redesign. It has a BRH section, editorial positioning, utility stats, sorting, category filters, masked articles, large cards, and an “Aussi dans l’actu” block, but the overall result feels dense and visually uneven. Some article entries are extremely long and repetitive, which makes the feed feel less curated and less premium.  ￼

Issues

The feed mixes too many content weights at once: long cards, stats, filters, utility blocks, and side content all compete for attention. The copy length on some items is excessive, and there is not enough distinction between a featured story, a standard story, a scholarship update, and a utility guide. The category and sorting controls also read more like raw controls than designed product elements.  ￼

Severity

Critical

Recommendations

Redesign this into an editorial system with three clear card types: hero, standard, and compact. Make the first screen much cleaner by reducing summary length and giving the page a dominant lead story plus supporting stories. Convert “Dernières / Pertinence / Bourses: deadline proche” into a proper segmented control. Redesign category chips so they feel substantial and color-coded by content type. Add stronger saved, hover, and quick-action states. The current page already has the right ingredients; it mostly needs a more disciplined layout system.  ￼

2) Homepage / — High

The homepage has a lot of useful content: a daily history item, latest news, trends, opportunities, Haiti, Monde, Éducation, Business, À la une, newsletter, and Instagram. That breadth is valuable, but too many modules currently sit at similar visual weight, so the page lacks a premium rhythm.  ￼

Issues

The homepage reads like a stack of content blocks rather than a true flagship landing experience. Key sections such as “Dernières nouvelles,” “Tendances,” “Opportunités,” and “À la une” do not feel distinct enough from each other. The current layout is informative, but it does not stage one clear narrative about what matters most today.  ￼

Severity

High

Recommendations

Create a true homepage hero with one flagship story and two supporting cards. Visually separate editorial sections from utility sections. The BRH and daily-history content should become premium data/story modules instead of plain stacked blocks. Alternate section backgrounds and vary card density to create pacing. Make the homepage feel more like a curated front page and less like an archive entry point.  ￼

3) Article detail template — High

I did not separately crawl a detail page in this pass, but the feed content strongly suggests the detail experience is central, especially for verified explainers, scholarship pages, and guides. Several summaries are long enough that the template likely carries a lot of structured reading burden.  ￼

Issues

The product appears to emphasize verification, sources, deadlines, and utility, which means the article template needs to feel trustworthy, structured, and easy to scan. Without a strong reading layout, the site risks feeling dense even when the content is valuable. This is especially important for long scholarship and guide content.  ￼

Severity

High

Recommendations

Use a larger title zone, cleaner meta row, stronger section breaks, and a sticky right rail on desktop for source, save, share, deadline, and related links. Build standard blocks for summary, eligibility, documents, costs, next steps, and official links. Add a related-content section at the end. This will make the product feel much more intentional and premium.  ￼

4) /ressources — High

This page has a good premise and useful framing: practical resources, guides, career, study abroad, history, and utility content. But visually it is too thin and too close to the main news feed. It has counts and sorting, but not enough identity.  ￼

Issues

The page signals “resource library,” but the current structure feels more like a standard list page. “21 ressources,” “19 formats utiles,” and “4 séries” are promising metrics, yet they do not appear to translate into a richer library experience.  ￼

Severity

High

Recommendations

Reframe the page as a library. Group resources into collections such as visa prep, scholarship search, applications, tests, budgeting, and student life. Use icon-backed tiles, starter kits, and “most useful this week” modules. Make this page denser in utility, but cleaner in presentation.  ￼

5) /succes — High

This page has strong emotional and brand potential, but it currently looks under-shaped. It positions itself around trajectories, leadership, and perseverance, yet the stats show 29 récits, 0 portraits, and 29 articles, which weakens the feeling of a distinctive storytelling surface.  ￼

Issues

For a page meant to inspire, the current structure appears too list-based. The absence of portraits is especially notable because this section should feel more human and image-led than the others.  ￼

Severity

High

Recommendations

Redesign it around spotlight stories, portrait cards, pull quotes, and thematic clusters. This page should feel warmer and more aspirational than /news. Use larger visuals, stronger author/subject emphasis, and a more magazine-like layout.  ￼

6) /bourses — Medium to High

This page is structurally one of the strongest on the site. It has a clear headline, deadline block, destination pathways, country filters, level filters, advanced filters, urgency labels, and source/verification links.  ￼

Issues

The information design is good, but the UI still feels more functional than premium. The deadline card, country pathways, and advanced filters all have strong product logic, but they need more design emphasis and clearer interaction states.  ￼

Severity

Medium to High

Recommendations

Turn the deadline section into a stronger countdown module. Make the destination pathways feel like rich tiles rather than plain category blocks. Upgrade the advanced filter area into a genuine product control panel with cleaner labels, spacing, and active states. Scholarship cards should also be cleaner, with better separation between eligibility, funding type, urgency, and official links.  ￼

7) /calendrier — Medium

The calendar page has a very strong concept: deadlines, exams, admissions, scholarships, and reminders in one place. It also has counts by Haiti vs international, plus category segmentation.  ￼

Issues

The page reads more like structured information than a polished planning tool. The framework is there, but the interface likely needs stronger date-based interaction and more vivid urgency patterns.  ￼

Severity

Medium

Recommendations

Make the calendar a true planning surface. Use clearer visual differentiation by event type, a stronger timeline or calendar grid, and better selected/hover states. Add “this week,” “upcoming,” and “urgent” treatments that are easy to scan. This page should feel more like a student control center.  ￼

8) /universites — Medium

This is probably the page with the best raw product potential. It already includes counts, countries, student-relevant framing, individual universities with location, cost band, languages, Haiti-friendliness markers, admissions links, scholarship links, and verification labels.  ￼

Issues

The page works, but it still feels database-like. Right now it looks more like a useful list than a premium comparison product. The tags and metadata are valuable, but visually compressed.  ￼

Severity

Medium

Recommendations

Upgrade each university into a true comparison card. Give stronger treatment to cost, language, city, and “HT” friendliness. Add save and compare interactions, and ideally a shortlist mode. This page is very close to becoming one of the best parts of the product.  ￼

9) /parcours — Medium

This page is very useful. It offers structured study-abroad pathways by country with steps, sources, and action logic. Canada, UK, U.S., and France are all presented as guided sequences.  ￼

Issues

The content is strong, but the presentation is still mostly sequential text. It reads like a written guide rather than an interactive pathway product.  ￼

Severity

Medium

Recommendations

Convert the steps into a visual roadmap with progress markers, icons, estimated timelines, and tags like tests, visa, funding, and deadlines. Add collapse/expand behavior and downloadable checklist logic. The underlying content is already good enough to support a much more premium interaction model.  ￼

10) /histoire — Low to Medium

The history page already has a stronger personality than some of the other sections. It includes a daily-history concept, monthly exploration, event counts, holidays, dates, imagery, importance framing, and source links.  ￼

Issues

It is differentiated conceptually, but it could still be much more visually memorable. A history page should feel ceremonial and immersive, not just informative.  ￼

Severity

Low to Medium

Recommendations

Lean harder into the editorial treatment. Make “Aujourd’hui dans l’histoire” feel like a hero feature. Improve date navigation, image framing, and event transitions. This page can become visually special without needing major structural work.  ￼

Cross-site issues

A) Header, footer, and global chrome — High

The same dense header/footer/navigation structure appears across the site, with many repeated top-level options and supporting links. That consistency is useful, but it also contributes to visual sameness.  ￼

Recommendation

Simplify the primary navigation and let the active page own more of the visual identity. Consider a more compact sticky top nav and a more structured footer with clearer grouping.

B) Card system — Critical

Nearly every page relies on cards, rows, or grouped blocks, but the system feels inconsistent in weight and behavior.  ￼

Recommendation

Create a single card library:
	•	hero card
	•	editorial card
	•	scholarship card
	•	university compare card
	•	utility guide card
	•	deadline card
	•	story/portrait card

This alone would sharply improve the site.

C) Typography and spacing — High

Across multiple pages, the text hierarchy is informative but not luxurious. Counts, chips, headings, and summaries often feel too similar in visual intensity.  ￼

Recommendation

Increase contrast between eyebrow labels, titles, metadata, body text, and utility tags. Use more whitespace around hero modules and section transitions.

D) Interaction states — High

The product clearly supports sorting, filtering, navigating, saving, and following deadlines, but the interaction model does not yet feel fully premium.  ￼

Recommendation

Improve hover, active, selected, saved, and focus states. Add tasteful motion for category switching, bookmarks, expanding details, and deadline urgency.

E) Page personality — High

The content pillars are distinct, but several pages still look too similar. News, resources, and success should not feel like the same feed with different titles.  ￼

Recommendation

Give each page family its own visual emphasis:
	•	/news: editorial and fast-moving
	•	/bourses: decision-oriented and urgent
	•	/calendrier: planning and time-based
	•	/universites: compare and shortlist
	•	/parcours: guided journey
	•	/succes: human and aspirational
	•	/histoire: archival and commemorative

What I would fix first in design terms

First, redesign the feed cards and overall visual hierarchy on /news. Second, rebuild the homepage as a real editorial front page. Third, create a shared design system for cards, chips, badges, filters, and saved states. Fourth, upgrade the scholarship, university, and pathway surfaces so they feel like polished tools, not just content pages.  ￼

Fastest wins

The fastest visible improvements would be:
	•	cut long preview copy everywhere
	•	create stronger category pills and badges
	•	introduce a more refined neutral palette with sharper accent usage
	•	add richer hover and saved states
	•	vary layout density between sections
	•	give each page a more distinct hero zone
	•	make deadlines and verification more visually elegant  ￼
