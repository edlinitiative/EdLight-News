Here is the page-by-page redesign audit, ordered by where polish will have the biggest impact first.

1) /news — update first

This is the page that most needs redesign. It has strong utility, but the feed feels dense, repetitive, and visually flat: long summaries, repeated metadata, high article volume, and limited distinction between item types. The page already contains useful mechanisms like BRH data, counts, filters, sort options, and topical chips, but the current presentation makes it feel more like an internal content list than a premium editorial product.  ￼

What is hurting the UI
	•	Article cards are too verbose.
	•	Summary text repeats itself in some entries.
	•	There is not enough difference between a major story, a utility resource, and a deadline-driven opportunity.
	•	Stats and filter controls are present, but they do not feel elevated or productized.  ￼

What to change
	•	Introduce 3 card sizes: hero story, standard story, compact row.
	•	Limit preview copy to one short dek.
	•	Add stronger category pills with distinct visual treatment for Haïti, Bourses, Ressources, Actualités.
	•	Make “Dernières / Pertinence / Deadline proche” into segmented controls with clear active state.
	•	Add hover lift, bookmark animation, and a “quick read” interaction.
	•	Use a more editorial layout: one lead story, two secondary cards, then feed rows.  ￼

Impact
This one change would improve the whole perceived quality of the product more than any other single redesign.  ￼

2) Homepage / — update second

The homepage has the right building blocks: latest news, trends, opportunities, Haiti, world, education, business, newsletter, and Instagram. The issue is that too many modules compete at the same visual weight, so the page lacks a premium rhythm and a strong landing-page narrative.  ￼

What is hurting the UI
	•	Too many sections with similar hierarchy.
	•	The home experience feels modular but not curated.
	•	The homepage does not visually “stage” what matters most today.
	•	Important modules such as “Dernières nouvelles,” “Tendances,” and “Opportunités” do not feel distinct enough.  ￼

What to change
	•	Create a real hero block at the top with one flagship story and supporting tiles.
	•	Turn the BRH exchange section into a compact premium financial card.
	•	Give each homepage band a different treatment: editorial, utility, urgent, discovery.
	•	Add more generous spacing and alternating section backgrounds.
	•	Use richer imagery or featured visual placeholders for key content areas.  ￼

Impact
The homepage would start to feel like a real media destination instead of a stacked directory of content.  ￼

3) Article detail pages — update third

The article template is useful, but the reading experience likely needs a more premium long-form structure. From the feed and linked content behavior, the product clearly leans on verified summaries, official links, and student-oriented synthesis; that should be reflected in a more deliberate reading layout.  ￼

What to change
	•	Increase title scale and improve spacing around metadata.
	•	Add a sticky side rail on desktop for source, share, save, and deadline.
	•	Separate “summary,” “eligibility,” “requirements,” and “next steps” into cleaner blocks.
	•	Add “related articles” and “similar opportunities” at the end.
	•	Introduce stronger trust markers: verified, updated, official source, last checked.  ￼

4) /ressources — update fourth

This page has a good premise, but visually it reads too much like a generic list. It has strong utility language, a clear purpose, counts, and sorting, but not enough identity of its own.  ￼

What is hurting the UI
	•	It lacks a “resource library” feel.
	•	The structure is too close to the news feed.
	•	The section deserves stronger grouping by practical use case.  ￼

What to change
	•	Reframe it as a library with collections.
	•	Group resources by theme: études, carrière, histoire, démarches, outils.
	•	Add collection cards like “Commencer ici,” “Étudier au Canada,” “Dossiers utiles.”
	•	Use icons and denser, more premium chips for type and difficulty.
	•	Add a “most useful this week” block.  ￼

5) /succes — update fifth

This page has a strong emotional role in the product, but the current structure looks thin for a page meant to inspire. It shows story counts and sorting, but the page does not yet feel like a premium storytelling destination.  ￼

What is hurting the UI
	•	“Portraits” shows as zero while “récits” and “articles” are populated, which weakens the page’s identity.
	•	The structure feels list-based instead of narrative-based.
	•	Inspiration content should feel warmer, more human, and more visual.  ￼

What to change
	•	Use larger spotlight cards with portrait-first treatment.
	•	Add quote pullouts and “key takeaway” badges.
	•	Create themed clusters: leadership, diaspora, academic success, perseverance.
	•	Make this page softer and more premium than the news feed.  ￼

6) /bourses — strong foundation, polish next

This page is one of the best structured ones already. It has a clear headline, a “deadline bientôt” area, destination pathways, filters by country and level, sorting, and tracked items. That is strong product thinking. What it needs most is visual elevation.  ￼

What is hurting the UI
	•	It feels functional, but not premium.
	•	Counts and stats are plain.
	•	Destination blocks could do much more visual work.
	•	Urgency states can feel more refined.  ￼

What to change
	•	Make stats into premium dashboard cards.
	•	Turn country pathways into rich destination tiles.
	•	Add visual countdown treatment for urgent deadlines.
	•	Improve follow/save states.
	•	Add compare mode for scholarships.
	•	Differentiate high-priority deadlines from evergreen opportunities.  ￼

7) /calendrier — strong utility, needs richer interaction

This page is directionally good. It clearly communicates deadlines, exam periods, admissions, and international items in one place, with tabs and category filters. The main issue is that it still feels like structured data more than a polished calendar product.  ￼

What is hurting the UI
	•	Calendar interactions are likely too restrained.
	•	Count cards and filters feel functional rather than premium.
	•	Urgency could be more visible and more intuitive.  ￼

What to change
	•	Create a richer visual timeline.
	•	Make exam, admissions, bourses, concours visually distinct.
	•	Add date-range hover and selected states.
	•	Use a dual-panel experience: calendar on one side, detail list on the other.
	•	Introduce stronger urgency patterns like countdown chips or “this week” highlights.  ￼

8) /universites — best candidate for a premium transformation

This page is probably the strongest raw product surface. It already includes country filters, count summaries, city, cost band, languages, Haitian friendliness markers, admissions, scholarships, and verification status. That is excellent structure.  ￼

What is hurting the UI
	•	The information density is strong, but cards need richer presentation.
	•	It likely feels like a database, not a premium comparison tool.
	•	It is missing the sense of exploration and confidence that students need.  ￼

What to change
	•	Upgrade each university into a proper comparison card.
	•	Add logo or visual identity for each institution.
	•	Separate cost, language, location, and Haitian-friendliness into clearly designed chips.
	•	Add save, compare, and shortlist interactions.
	•	Add a “best for” label such as budget, bilingual, top-ranked, easier access.
	•	On desktop, allow side-by-side compare.  ￼

Impact
This page could become one of the most distinctive parts of the whole product with relatively little structural change.  ￼

9) /parcours — useful but too text-sequential

This page has good strategic content: country-specific pathways, step counts, and ordered guidance. The weakness is presentation. It reads more like a text guide than an interactive roadmap.  ￼

What is hurting the UI
	•	It relies heavily on sequential text blocks.
	•	There is not enough visual momentum from one step to the next.
	•	It does not yet feel like a guided journey.  ￼

What to change
	•	Convert steps into a visual stepper.
	•	Add timeline estimates per step.
	•	Tag each step with cost, documents, tests, difficulty, and expected lead time.
	•	Let users collapse or expand each phase.
	•	Add downloadable checklist behavior.  ￼

10) /histoire — lower priority, but visually important

This page already has a stronger identity than some others because it is inherently thematic: historical events, national commemorations, monthly exploration, and day-based navigation. Still, it could feel much more special visually.  ￼

What is hurting the UI
	•	It needs stronger visual storytelling.
	•	“Éphéméride” content should feel ceremonial, not just informational.
	•	The date navigation can likely do more work.  ￼

What to change
	•	Build a real “today in history” hero.
	•	Add archive-style month navigation.
	•	Use stronger image framing and timeline styling.
	•	Highlight national holidays and major figures with distinctive badges.
	•	Introduce motion or transitions when switching dates.  ￼

Cross-site design issues to fix everywhere

Several problems repeat across nearly all pages: the same top navigation and footer system appear everywhere, many pages rely on similar stat blocks and text-heavy sections, and the overall experience appears strong on information architecture but lighter on visual depth, interaction richness, and page-specific personality.  ￼

The biggest global fixes
	•	Build a real component system: hero cards, feed cards, utility cards, compare cards, deadline cards, step cards.
	•	Add stronger surface hierarchy with more depth and contrast.
	•	Tighten typography so titles, dek text, metadata, and chips feel more premium.
	•	Improve hover, active, selected, and saved states.
	•	Give each page family its own visual identity while staying inside one design system.  ￼
